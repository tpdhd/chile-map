📍 One-Way Routes & Drop-Off Fees

⚠️ One-way fees are expensive in Chile The industry standard is approximately USD $0.45 per km. All companies charge similar rates — there's no way to avoid it. But for a long country like Chile, one-way makes the most sense.
💡 Pro Tip: Rent in Santiago, drop in Puerto Montt This is the sweet spot. The drop-off fee is ~$390 USD — much cheaper than going all the way to Patagonia ($1,290). Santiago→Puerto Montt on Ruta 5 is an excellent, fully-paved 4-lane highway.

✅ Yes, you can rent at Santiago SCL Airport and drop at Puerto Montt PMO Airport.

📋 Required Documents

Required

  • Valid passport — must be valid throughout rental period
  • Driver's license from your home country — must be readable in Latin script
  • Credit card in renter's name — Visa, MasterCard, Amex, or Diners Club. No debit or prepaid cards!
  • Booking confirmation — printed or digital

Recommended

  • International Driving Permit (IDP) — not strictly required for German/EU licenses, but recommended as backup. Required if your license is in non-Latin script (Chinese, Arabic, etc.)
  • Second credit card — some companies require high guarantee deposits
  • Travel insurance documentation
🇩🇪 For German/EU License Holders Your German Führerschein is accepted in Chile for up to 90 days (tourist visa duration). An IDP is nice to have but not required. The U.S. Embassy in Chile confirms: "Visitors can drive with a valid license for the duration of their tourist permit."
💳 Credit Card Guarantee Deposits Companies block a significant amount on your credit card:
• Economy/Compact: CLP 500,000 (~€490)
• Midsize/Crossover: CLP 700,000 (~€685)
• SUV: CLP 900,000 (~€880)
• Pickup 4×4: CLP 1,500,000–2,000,000 (~€1,470–1,960)
Make sure your credit card limit can handle this!

🛡️ Insurance

What's Included (Standard)

Most Chilean rental companies include basic insurance in the rate:

  • CDW (Collision Damage Waiver) — covers damage to the rental car with a deductible
  • DAT/Third-Party Liability — covers damage to other vehicles/property
  • Theft protection — with deductible

Deductibles (Your Responsibility Per Incident)

The deductible is per accident, not per rental:

  • Economy/Compact: CLP 500,000 (~$525 USD)
  • Midsize/Crossover: CLP 700,000 (~$735 USD)
  • SUV/Pickup: CLP 900,000 (~$945 USD)

Optional Upgrades

  • Full coverage (zero deductible) — eliminates deductible entirely. Costs ~$10–20/day extra
  • Tire/windshield coverage — standard CDW often excludes tires, wheels, and windshield
  • Roadside assistance upgrade — enhanced towing coverage (important for remote areas)
⚠️ Insurance Coverage vs. Road Type Insurance companies only cover damage when the vehicle is appropriate for the road. If you rent an economy car and drive it on gravel/dirt roads in Patagonia, damage may NOT be covered. For Ruta 5 (Santiago→Puerto Montt), any car is fine.
💡 Tip: Start in Santiago for better insurance LYS recommends renting in Santiago (not Patagonia) even for south-bound trips. Reason: rates are lower AND insurance deductibles are lower than rentals starting in Patagonia.

🛣️ Road Conditions: Ruta 5

Ruta 5 (Pan-American Highway): Santiago → Puerto Montt

Excellent condition. Modern, well-maintained 4-lane divided highway for the entire 1,020 km stretch. Comparable to European motorways.

  • Fully paved, 4 lanes (2 each direction) with median barrier
  • Speed limit: 120 km/h
  • Regular Copec gas stations with "Pronto" rest stops (food, coffee, bathrooms)
  • Multiple toll plazas along the way (manual, cash/card)
  • Well-marked with good signage
  • Any car type is suitable — no need for SUV or 4×4

Carretera Austral (Ruta 7): Puerto Montt → South

Mixed conditions — proceed with caution. Beautiful but challenging. Only attempt with proper vehicle.

  • Mix of asphalt, gravel, dirt, and volcanic ash
  • 2-lane, narrow in places, no median
  • Limited services — plan fuel stops carefully
  • SUV or pickup truck strongly recommended
  • Carry spare tire kit, air compressor, and emergency supplies
  • Some sections require ferry crossings
📱 Navigation Download offline maps (Google Maps or Maps.me) before leaving Santiago. Cell coverage is good on Ruta 5 and in towns, but can be spotty between cities and in mountains. Waze works well in Chile.

🏗️ Tolls & TAG System

Santiago Urban Highways — TAG Required

Six major highways in Santiago use mandatory electronic TAG transponders. No cash option. All rental cars include TAG — confirm at pickup.

  • Autopista Central, Costanera Norte, Vespucio Norte/Sur, Túnel San Cristóbal, Autopista 68
  • Cost: CLP 2,000–5,000 per highway section (~$2–5 USD)
  • Without TAG: $12 USD day-pass fee per highway used
  • TAG charges added to your rental invoice after return

Intercity Ruta 5 — Manual Toll Booths

Traditional toll plazas with booths. Cash (Chilean pesos) strongly recommended. Some accept Visa/MC.

  • Each plaza: CLP 800–4,500 (~$1–5 USD)
  • Santiago to Puerto Montt total: CLP 40,000–60,000 (~$40–60 USD)
  • Multiple plazas along the route (approximately every 80–100 km)
  • Weekend surcharge: 15–20% higher on Fridays afternoon through Sunday
💡 Carry CLP 60,000–80,000 in cash for tolls Even though some plazas accept cards, cash is faster and always accepted. Get Chilean pesos at SCL airport or ATM before departing Santiago.

Essential Rules for Tourists

  • Drive on the RIGHT side of the road
  • Headlights must be ON at ALL times — even during daytime
  • Seatbelts mandatory for all occupants
  • NO right turn on red — unless specifically signed (very rare)
  • NO mobile phone while driving (hands-free only)
  • NO smoking while driving — it's illegal
  • NO eating while driving
  • Pedestrians always have right of way at crossings
  • Speed cameras and radar guns are frequent — fines are steep
  • Never offer money to police — Chilean police (Carabineros) are honest and rarely corrupt
⚠️ Alcohol: Extremely strict Chile's BAC limit is 0.3 g/L — that's about one small beer. Heavy fines and possible imprisonment. Don't risk it. The safest approach: zero alcohol if driving.
🚔 If stopped by Carabineros Be polite and respectful. Keep documents ready (license, passport, rental papers — should be in the glove box). Tourists who are friendly almost never get tickets. There are regular checkpoints on highways.

Santiago-Specific Rules

  • Bus-only lanes (yellow median) — DO NOT use them, enforced by cameras
  • Reversible lanes on main roads change direction during rush hours
  • Vehicle restrictions — during high pollution days, cars with certain license plate numbers are banned from 7:30 AM–9:00 PM (mainly affects older cars without catalytic converters; rental cars typically exempt)
  • Narrow parking spaces — get a compact car for Santiago

🚫 Restrictions

Where You Can't Take Rental Cars

  • Peru and Bolivia — completely prohibited. Cars get stolen, and diplomatic relations are complicated
  • Argentina — possible WITH advance authorization (5 working days), extra permit fee (USD 90–290), and extra insurance. Not all companies allow it
  • Off-road driving — not permitted with standard rental. Insurance won't cover damage
  • Unpaved roads with economy cars — technically allowed, but insurance won't cover damage if vehicle isn't suitable for the road
🇦🇷 Planning to cross to Argentina? Notify your rental company at booking time. You'll need: passport photos + license copies of all drivers sent in advance. If license and passport are from different countries, an IDP is required. Extra fee: $90–290 USD. No roadside assistance outside Chile.

🏆 Pro Tips for the Santiago → South Road Trip

🗓️ Planning

  • Book early for Feb/Mar — this is peak summer season in Chile
  • Check Transfercar first — if there's a free relocation deal matching your dates, take it
  • Compare 3+ platforms — prices vary significantly between aggregators
  • Consider rental length — one-way drop-off fee stays fixed; longer rentals make it proportionally cheaper
  • Travel north→south recommended by LYS: better rates, better insurance starting from Santiago

💰 Saving Money

  • Manual transmission is significantly cheaper than automatic in Chile
  • Compact car is all you need for Ruta 5 — save $15–30/day vs. SUV
  • Drop off in Puerto Montt (~$400 fee) instead of Punta Arenas (~$1,300 fee) — fly PMO→SCL cheaply
  • Book direct with local companies (Chilean, Rosselot) — sometimes no middleman markup
  • Get cash for tolls — avoid premium card charges at booths

🚗 On the Road

  • Fill up at every Copec station — don't let tank go below half, especially heading south
  • Copec "Pronto" = your best friend — great coffee, food, and bathrooms on the highway
  • Carry Chilean pesos — small towns, tolls, and parking often cash-only
  • Download offline maps — Google Maps lets you download regions for offline use
  • Don't drive at night in rural areas — animals on road, poor lighting, fatigued truck drivers
  • Check vehicle thoroughly at pickup — photograph every scratch and dent
  • Keep all documents in car — rental contract, license, passport. Regular police checkpoints on highways
  • Parking: look for the assistant — "cuidadores de autos" watch your car for a small tip (CLP 500–1,000)

🍽️ Road Trip Food & Culture

  • Chileans eat dinner late (after 8:30 PM) — plan accordingly
  • Chile = amazing seafood, fresh fruit, and wine
  • Tip 10% at restaurants
  • Few people speak English outside Santiago — learn basic Spanish
  • Carry only new/clean USD or EUR bills for exchange — old/torn bills are rejected
  • Don't leave anything visible in the car — use the trunk
